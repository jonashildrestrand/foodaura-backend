## Feature Details

### User Profile & Goals
**Description:** A setup flow where the user provides personal details (age, weight, height, activity level) and their primary goal (e.g. build muscle, lose weight, eat better). This profile drives nutritional targets used throughout the app.
**User value:** Ensures every meal plan and recipe recommendation is calibrated to the individual, not a generic default.
**Notes:** Nutrition is presented as context, not a scoreboard — avoid surfacing raw calorie/macro targets unless the user explicitly seeks them.

---

### Household Profiles
**Description:** Users can invite others to join their household. Each member has their own Foodaura account, name, goals, and nutritional profile. Invitations can be sent to existing users or to new users who create an account on signup.
**User value:** Enables planning for the whole household without cooking separate meals or managing separate apps.
**Notes:** This is a key differentiator — most competitors plan for one person only.

---

### AI Meal Plan Generation
**Description:** One-tap generation of a full weekly meal plan that accounts for every household member's nutritional profile and goals. The user sets the week and the app handles the rest.
**User value:** Eliminates the mental overhead of figuring out what to eat — going from goals to a full weekly plan in under 2 minutes.
**Notes:** Should be re-generatable (e.g. "regenerate this day") without losing the rest of the plan.

---

### Manual Meal Planning
**Description:** A drag-and-drop or slot-based interface where users can assign recipes to any meal slot in the week, bypassing or overriding AI suggestions.
**User value:** Gives control to users who have preferences the AI can't know — a recipe they want to use up, a tradition, or a specific craving.
**Notes:** Manual planning should still show nutritional context per slot so users can make informed choices.

---

### Recipe Discovery
**Description:** A browsable and searchable recipe catalogue, filtered by the user's profile and goals by default. Users can adjust filters (cuisine, cook time, ingredients) but the default view is already relevant to them.
**User value:** Removes the work of manually filtering hundreds of irrelevant recipes to find one that fits.
**Notes:** Non-goal: social or community-submitted recipes. Curated catalogue only.

---

### Recipe Scaling
**Description:** Recipes automatically adjust ingredient quantities based on who is eating that meal — the full household on shared nights, or a subset of members on separate-eating days.
**User value:** Eliminates manual math when cooking for different numbers of people, and ensures each person gets the right amount for their goals.
**Notes:** Scaling should respect per-person nutritional targets, not just multiply servings evenly.

---

### Automatic Shopping List
**Description:** The meal plan generates a consolidated, de-duplicated shopping list that covers all recipes for the week. Quantities are combined (e.g. "500g chicken" across three recipes becomes one line item).
**User value:** Removes the need to manually cross-reference recipes and build a list by hand — the most tedious step in meal planning.
**Notes:** List should be organizable by category (produce, protein, pantry) to match how people shop.

---

### Household Scheduling
**Description:** Users mark which members are home and eating together on each day of the week. The meal plan and recipe scaling adapt accordingly — shared meals on some nights, individual portions on others.
**User value:** Reflects how households actually live, rather than assuming everyone always eats together.
**Notes:** The schedule can be set once as a template (e.g. "Monday I eat alone, Friday we eat together") and adjusted per week.

---

### Nutritional Context
**Description:** Each meal and day shows a simple nutritional summary — protein, carbs, fat, and calories — as supporting context for the plan. Not a tracking interface; users can't log against it.
**User value:** Gives users enough visibility to feel confident their plan supports their goals, without turning food into a numbers game.
**Notes:** Display should be calm and ambient, not prominent. The plan itself is the product; nutrition is the explanation behind it.

---

### Recipe Preferences
**Description:** Users can like or dislike recipes — either after eating them or when browsing based on ingredients they don't enjoy. Preferences are stored per household and influence recipe discovery and AI meal plan generation (disliked recipes are excluded; liked recipes are favoured).
**User value:** The app learns what the household actually enjoys over time, making meal plans progressively more relevant without any manual curation effort.
**Notes:** Preferences are stored per user, not per household — because a user may belong to multiple households, preferences should carry across all of them. When planning for a multi-member household, all members' preferences are visible to the planner so they can make informed decisions. Preferences are never exposed outside households the user belongs to. This is not a social or rating feature; it's a personalisation signal. Ingredient-level dislikes (e.g. "I don't like coriander") can be handled here or surfaced as part of the user profile setup.

---

### Meal Plan History
**Description:** Users can view past weekly meal plans and re-use any previous week as a starting point for a new one.
**User value:** Once a household finds a week that works, they don't want to start from scratch every time — history lets them repeat good weeks with one tap.
**Notes:** P2 — valuable once users have been active for 4+ weeks.

---

### Notifications
**Description:** In-app notification centre where users see alerts for household events (invitations, member changes) and meal plan updates (AI plan ready). Notifications are stored in the database and marked read/unread in the app.
**User value:** Keeps users informed of changes that affect them without requiring them to actively check — an invitation doesn't get missed, and a generated meal plan is surfaced immediately.
**Notes:** Delivery is in-app only for now. The schema is designed so a separate notification service can read the table in the future and dispatch notifications via email, SMS, or push — without changes to the backend. The backend only writes notifications; it does not own delivery.
