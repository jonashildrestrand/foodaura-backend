# Brand & Voice

Foodaura is a food companion for households who want to eat with purpose. It turns each person's health goals into a shared weekly meal plan and a consolidated shopping list — without calorie tracking, restrictive diets, or hours of manual planning.

**It is not** a calorie tracker, a diet enforcer, or a meal kit service. Nutrition is shown as calm, supporting context — never a scoreboard.

---

## Who We're For

**The Household Planner (25–40)**
Manages cooking and shopping for two or more people. Wants intention without overhead. Doesn't want to coordinate three different apps for three people with three different diets.

**The Goal-Conscious Single (22–35)**
Has a fitness goal but finds calorie trackers obsessive and clinical. Wants eating to feel purposeful, not surveilled.

Both users share one need: a plan that does the thinking so they don't have to, while still feeling like theirs.

---

## Voice

Foodaura speaks like a thoughtful housemate who happens to be good at planning. Calm. Practical. Warm without being chirpy.

### The four rules

**Calm and grounded.** No exclamation marks. No "Woohoo!" No "Let's crush this week." The app is confident that your plan is good — it doesn't need to perform enthusiasm about it.

**Practical.** Tell the user what's happening or what to do, in the fewest plain words. If the app needs three sentences to say something, something is wrong.

**Warm, not cute.** Food is enjoyable, not gamified. Avoid emoji confetti, achievement language, streaks, and badges. A notification is not an opportunity to celebrate.

**Trustworthy.** The user is delegating planning to us. Copy should feel considered — like advice from someone who thought about it — not chatty filler.

## Language Rules

### Person & address
- Use **"you"** to address the user directly.
- We don't say "we" often — the app is a tool, not a buddy.
- Foodaura refers to itself in third person only when introducing a capability: "Foodaura builds your plan from…"
- Household members are referred to by name wherever possible.

### Casing
- **Sentence case** everywhere: headings, buttons, tabs, menu items. ("Generate plan", not "Generate Plan".)
- **Title case** only for proper nouns and recipe names. Recipe names follow their catalogue casing (*Miso-Glazed Salmon Bowl*).
- **ALL CAPS** is reserved for tiny eyebrow labels at small sizes with letter-spacing (`THIS WEEK`, `MON`). Never for full phrases.

### Verbs we use
- **Generate plan** / **Regenerate** (not "Create meal plan")
- **Swap recipe** (not "Replace", not "Change")
- **Add to plan** (not "Schedule", not "Assign")
- **Like** / **Dislike** for recipe preferences (not "Save", "Bookmark", or "Heart")
- **Shopping list**, not "Cart" — items are **checked off**, not "completed"
- **Mark as eaten** (not "Log meal")

### Nutrition language
- Nutrition is shown with **labels first, numbers quiet**: "Protein 32g · Carbs 48g · Fat 14g" — never giant percentage rings or red-flag colour coding.
- Calories appear as a small supporting figure ("520 kcal"), never as the headline of a meal or a day.
- Quantities are the scaled result, stated plainly: "320g chicken thighs" — never "(scaled from 1 to 2.7 servings)".

## Microcopy Patterns

**Empty states are inviting, not alarming.**
- "No plan yet — generate one." not "You have no plan."
- "Nothing in your shopping list yet." not "Shopping list is empty!"

**Confirmations are quiet.**
A subtle inline toast ("Plan saved.") not a modal celebration. The event speaks for itself.

**Errors are calm.**
- "Couldn't generate this day. Try again?" not "Error 500: internal server error."
- Explain what happened and offer a path forward. Never expose stack traces or codes.

**Invitations are warm.**
- "They'll receive a one-time link. Their preferences will fold into your household's plan."

---

## What We Never Do

- Use emoji in product UI (user-entered free text is the only exception)
- Use achievement language, streaks, badges, or scores
- Show red or alarming states for neutral nutritional information
- Use unicode glyphs (★ ✓ →) in place of icons
- Use blue-tinged colour in any surface, border, or type
- Write exclamation marks in system-generated copy
